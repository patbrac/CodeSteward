## Summary

Explain the proposed public-contract change and intended user outcome in a short paragraph.

## Motivation and evidence

Describe the observed problem, affected users, evidence, current workaround, and why existing contracts are insufficient. Separate facts from assumptions.

## Proposal

Specify behavior, scope, defaults, invariants, ownership, and what is deliberately excluded. Include enough detail to assess compatibility and conformance.

## Alternatives considered

Include the status quo and credible alternatives, with benefits, costs, and reasons not selected.

## Security impact

Cover trust boundaries, hostile inputs, execution/network capabilities, secrets, abuse, supply chain, mitigations, and residual risk.

## Privacy and data impact

Cover source/data collection, transmission, persistence, retention, deletion, access, logging, and consent. State whether defaults change.

## Compatibility and cross-platform impact

Cover CLI/config/schema/API changes, migrations, deterministic output, native Windows/macOS/Linux behavior, support windows, integrations, and plugins.

## Open-core, license, and governance impact

Explain effects on contributor/user rights, open versus paid capabilities, trademarks, decision authority, and the immutable product contract.

## Evidence and acceptance criteria

List falsifiable tests, measurements, required native targets, review evidence, and artifact locations. Do not treat RFC acceptance as implementation proof.

## Rollout, migration, and rollback

Define sequencing, previews/flags, documentation, telemetry (if any), migration tools, deprecation period, failure thresholds, recovery, and rollback.

## Supersession

State which prior contract/RFC this replaces, which conditions would trigger reconsideration, how users migrate, and what a future superseding RFC must preserve or explicitly change.
